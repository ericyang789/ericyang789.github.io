Lung Lesion Diagnosis

Spring 2021

Deep learning models for lung lesion classification

Lung Lesion Diagnosis

The early identification of lung lesions is very important as lesions have a high likelihood of becoming cancerous. An accurate, computer-aided diagnosis model could improve workflow prioritization, support decision making in clinical settings, and ultimately help progress large-scale screening and global population health initiatives. Towards this, my peers and I developed and tuned deep learning models for automated chest radiograph interpretation of lung lesions with high performance (>0.8 AUROC). Leveraging popular Python packages and deep learning frameworks such as Scikit-Learn, Pandas, Numpy, Keras, and TensorFlow, we also explored the implications of class imbalance and dataset cleanliness in deep learning model development.

Adverse Events Viz

Spring 2021

RShiny app for adverse events data visualization

Adverse Events Viz

The adverse events dataset is compiled by the U.S. FDA where reports from consumers about adverse events involving in-market products (foods, dietary supplements and cosmetics) are collected. With thousands of reports dating back to 2004 recorded in tabular form, there is a need for a visualization platform to perform exploratory analyses on this large data set. Using RShiny, tidyverse and Plotly packages, my peer and I created a reactive dashboard that allows user customization o linked graphical displays of the adverse events data. The flexible application enables different subsets, facets and groups based on victim demographic and product categories to help users observe trends in the database.

Parallelized t-SNE

Spring 2021

Big Compute: Parallelized t-SNE for Dimensionality Reduction

Parallelized t-SNE

t-Distrubuted Stochastic Neighbor Embedding (t-SNE) is a popular dimensionality reduction technique for visualizing large, high-dimensional datasets in low-dimensional space. Motivated by customizable parallelizability in lower level programming languages, my peers and I implemented the first t-SNE algorithm in C to our knowledge. In addition, we parallelized our software with OpenACC directives to leverage GPU computing and achieved significant speedup compared to popular Python implementations.

Logic Degradable Nanogels

Summer 2018 - Spring 2020

Chemotherapeutics-delivering biomaterials platform

Logic Degradable Nanogels

For my undergraduate thesis at the UW, I developd logic degradable nanogels at the DeForest Research Group . The delivery of cell and drug-based chemotherapeutics to tumors have presented major challenges in effective cancer treatment. Towards this, I designed a chemical framework for creating user-programmable nanogels that undergo programmed degradation in response to multiple environmental cues following Boolean logic. User-specified combinations of environmental inputs (e.g., tumor-presented enzymes, reducing conditions) yield material breakdown, thereby triggering therapeutic release. Through this work, I have been awarded two research scholarships and presented my studies at two professional research symposiums.

Thermostable β-Glucanase

Winter 2020

Computational design of beer-brewing protein

Thermostable β-Glucanase

Protein design has become more and more computational. Here, I designed a thermostable β-Glucanase enzyme for beer brewing. Due to high temperatures during brewing workflows, there is tremendous need in developing a β-Glucanase variant that can maximize enzyme efficacy at high temperatures. With Rosetta, Chimera and intuition about protein structure and functions, I successfully designed an enzyme with such properties, with the results verified by Molecular Dynamics Simulations via Bash scripting.

3D Arteriole Capillary Venule Unit

Spring 2017 - Winter 2020

Engineered vessels to study malaria-infected red blood cells

3D Arteriole Capillary Venule Unit

Serving as a researcher in the DeForest Research Group at the UW, I assisted in engineering a 3D arteriole-capillary-venule unit with unprecedented resolution to study malaria- infected red blood cells. To process large time series datasets generated by transmission electron microscopes during blood flow experiments, I developed image and statistical analysis software in Matlab and used these computational scripts to investigate cell-vessel interactions. Throughout the project, not only did I acquire invaluable expertise in tissue engineering and infectious disease, I was also exposed to biocomputational algorithms that are widely used today. Furthermore, I was fortunate to publish my work in Science Advances.

Anophelseize

Winter 2017 - Winter 2020

Male mosquito capturing device

Anophelseize

Taking on one of the Grand Challenges in Global Health issued by the Bill & Melinda Gates Foundation, I co-founded Anophelseize. Utilizing acoustic and chemo attractants, Anophelseize is an efficient, low-cost capturing device for male mosquitoes of the species Anopheles gambiae to be used for field malaria research. Prototypes were generated with AutoCAD, 3D printing, laser cutting and other fabrication techniques. The team secured $2000+ in prototype funding and exceled in business-health competitions. This unique, collaborative project has inspired me to further my career in biomedical informatics by opening my eyes to the importance of computational sciences in global health, given the complex large datasets that transcend national borders.

PID Responsive Blanket

Analog ODE Solver

Spring 2018

Analog computer design

Analog ODE Solver

Although rarely used in today’s world, analog computers can be extremely useful in solving mathematical operations and differential equations. Using electrical engineering principals, I designed and built an analog computer using a combination of circuits to solve an ordinary differential equation modeling glucose transport and metabolism within a cell with my peers. Matlab modeling and analytical calculus were used to verify the analog computers’ solutions obtained from oscilloscopes.

EKG Data Processing
