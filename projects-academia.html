Cancer Classifier

Spring 2020

For the final project of my Genome Informatics course at the UW, I constructed a machine learning model for malignant vs benign cancer classification. I optimized a random forest model trained on digitalized images of a fine needle aspirate from breast masses. Leveraging popular Python packages such as Scikit-Learn, Pandas, Numpy, and Scipy to tune model hyperparameters, I achieved high classification accuracies for my training and validation datasets.

Logic Degradable Nanogels

Summer 2018 - Spring 2020

For my undergraduate thesis at the UW, I am currently developing logic degradable nanogels at the DeForest Research Group . The delivery of cell and drug-based chemotherapeutics to tumors have presented major challenges in effective cancer treatment. Towards this, I have developed a chemical framework for creating user-programmable nanogels that undergo programmed degradation in response to multiple environmental cues following Boolean logic. User-specified combinations of environmental inputs (e.g., tumor-presented enzymes, reducing conditions) yield material breakdown, thereby triggering therapeutic release. Through this work, I have been awarded two research scholarships and presented my studies at two professional research symposiums.

Thermostable β-Glucanase

Winter 2020

For the final project of my Computational Protein Design course at the UW, I designed a thermostable β-Glucanase enzyme for beer brewing. Due to high temperatures during brewing workflows, there is tremendous need in developing a β-Glucanase variant that can maximize enzyme efficacy at high temperatures. With Rosetta and Chimera, I successfully designed an enzyme with such properties, with the results verified by Molecular Dynamics Simulations via Bash scripting.

3D Arteriole-Capillary-Venule Unit

Spring 2017 - Winter 2020

For my first project in the DeForest Research Group at the UW, I assisted in engineering a 3D arteriole-capillary-venule unit with unprecedented resolution to study malaria- infected red blood cells. To process large datasets generated by transmission electron microscopes during real-time blood flow experiments, I developed image and statistical analysis software in Matlab and used these computational scripts to investigate cell-vessel interactions. Throughout the project, not only did I acquire invaluable expertise in tissue engineering and infectious disease, I was also exposed to biocomputational algorithms that are widely used today. Furthermore, I was fortunate to publish my work in Science Advances.

Anophelseize

Winter 2017 - Winter 2020

Taking on one of the Grand Challenges in Global Health launched by the Bill & Melinda Gates Foundation, I co-founded Anophelseize. Utilizing acoustic and chemo attractants, Anophelseize is an efficient, low-cost capturing device for male mosquitoes of the species Anopheles gambiae to be used for field malaria research. Prototypes were generated with AutoCAD, 3D printing, laser cutting and other fabrication techniques. The team secured $2000+ in prototype funding and exceled in business-health competitions. This unique, collaborative project has inspired me to further my studies in biomedical informatics by opening my eyes to the importance of computational sciences in global health, given the complex large datasets that transcend national borders.

PID Hypothermia Blanket Simulation

Winter 2019

As hypothermia kills twice as many people as heat does in the US, there is tremendous need in developing an active, modular, portable heating blanket to be used before emergency room arrival. In my Mass Transport and Systems course at the UW, I developed a PID controlled hypothermia blanket model using LabView simulations and basic circuitry along with my peers. The group was able to achieve a 2 degree/min temperature increase by tuning the PID parameters.

Low Cost Spirometer

Autumn 2018

A spirometer is a medical device used by physicians, patients and researchers to observe lung functionality. In my Fluids and Materials course at the UW, my peers and I designed and built a low-cost spirometer based on water displacement and pressure sensing. We fabricated the prototype, hand crafted a pressure gauge with circuitry, scripted the display interface with Java, and modeled our design with calculus and COMSOL.

Analog ODE Solver

Spring 2018

Although rarely used in today’s world, analog computers can be extremely useful in solving mathematical operations and differential equations. In my Biomedical Signals and Sensors course at the UW, I designed and built an analog computer using a combination of circuits to solve an ordinary differential equation modeling glucose transport and metabolism within a cell with my peers. Matlab modeling and analytical calculus were used to verify the analog computers’ solutions obtained from oscilloscopes.

Electrocardiogram Data Processing

Spring 2018

For a project in my Biomedical Signal and Sensors course at the UW, I processed EKG signals end-to-end from acquisition to signal visualization along with my peers. With voltage input from 7 electrodes on a live human subject, a lot of noisy data were generated. Using Matlab, running average filters and other signal processing algorithms were applied in order to visualize EKG metrics such as heart rate and peak shapes.
